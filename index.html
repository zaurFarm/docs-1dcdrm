What Makes Rolex's New 'Jubilee Gold' so Special?

Rolex has a history of developing proprietary materials. Everose gold, introduced in 2005, was the brand's patented rose-gold alloy designed to resist fading. Oystersteel is their name for 904L stainless steel. In 2026, the pattern continues with Jubilee Gold — a new 18-karat alloy created specifically for the centennial.

The metallurgy

Jubilee Gold is a warm-toned alloy that sits between traditional yellow gold and Everose. Rolex has not published the exact composition, but spectroscopic analysis by independent watchmakers suggests a higher copper content than standard yellow gold with trace additions of platinum-group elements for oxidation resistance. The result is a color that reads as honey-gold in daylight and deepens to amber under incandescent light.

Where it appears in the lineup

For now, Jubilee Gold is exclusive to three references in the centennial Oyster collection and two Datejust variants. Rolex has historically expanded proprietary materials across the catalog over two to three years following introduction — Everose followed exactly this pattern — so expect Jubilee Gold Day-Dates, GMT-Masters, and possibly a Daytona within the next release cycle.

Why it matters to collectors

A proprietary alloy means Rolex controls the supply chain from foundry to finished case. No other brand can produce this color, and no aftermarket refinisher can replicate it. That exclusivity is the point.

First-year Jubilee Gold pieces carry the same centennial cachet as the anniversary Oyster. Collectors who missed the steel centennial models are targeting these as the next best entry point into the anniversary narrative.
